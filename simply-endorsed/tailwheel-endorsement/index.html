Why tailwheel is grouped with other aircraft endorsements

Tailwheel is not a standalone world inside AC 61-65K. The aircraft-endorsement section also includes complex, high-performance, and high-altitude signoffs. Simply Endorsed groups those items because instructors often move through them as a family of operating endorsements even when the pilot is only searching for one specific item.

For the tailwheel case, the key endorsement is A.75. It documents that the pilot received the required training and was found proficient to act as PIC in a tailwheel airplane. The adjacent entries matter because they help pilots avoid assuming that one aircraft endorsement automatically covers another. Tailwheel is separate from complex, high-performance, and high-altitude privileges.

FAQ: tailwheel endorsements

Does the tailwheel endorsement expire?
No. The endorsement documents the required training and proficiency for acting as PIC in a tailwheel airplane. Pilots still need to maintain overall currency and proficiency, but the endorsement itself is not written as a temporary 90-day authorization.
Does a complex or high-performance endorsement cover tailwheel?
No. Tailwheel is a separate endorsement. That is one reason the aircraft-endorsement group is useful: it shows those signoffs side by side without implying they substitute for each other.
Why does this guide open the full aircraft-endorsement group instead of a page with only A.75?
Because tailwheel lives inside the broader aircraft-endorsement group in Simply Endorsed. The deep link narrows the live search to tailwheel while keeping the pilot in the correct category context.
Can sport-pilot instructors ever appear in the tailwheel context?
AC 61-65K notes that the endorsement can be given in a sport pilot aircraft by a sport pilot instructor or in an airplane by a flight instructor with a rating other than a sport pilot rating. Instructors still need to verify the exact context that applies.
