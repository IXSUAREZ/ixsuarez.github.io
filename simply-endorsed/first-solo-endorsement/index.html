Simply Endorsed First Solo Endorsement Guide

The first-solo path in AC 61-65K usually means more than one endorsement. In most airplane training scenarios, the instructor is confirming that the student passed the required pre-solo knowledge test, received the required pre-solo flight training, and is now authorized for solo flight in that make and model for the first 90-calendar-day period.

That is why the first-solo bundle inside Simply Endorsed groups A.3, A.4, and A.6 together. The tool also surfaces A.14 as the common supplemental item when the instructor is using the TSA citizenship endorsement method instead of keeping copies of the citizenship documents. For a student pilot or a busy CFI, that bundle view is much easier to scan than flipping through AC 61-65 and cross-checking the student-solo section every time the lesson gets close to solo.

What the first-solo endorsement path is doing

First solo is one of the clearest examples of why instructors need a structured endorsement reference. A student does not move from “almost ready” to “solo authorized” through one line in the logbook. The instructor is documenting academic preparation under 14 CFR 61.87(b), flight training and proficiency under 14 CFR 61.87(c), and the actual initial solo authorization under 14 CFR 61.87(n). Each part answers a different question: did the student learn the rules and procedures, did the student demonstrate the required maneuvers safely, and is the student authorized to act alone in that make and model right now?

Simply Endorsed packages those items the way instructors actually think about them in training. If a student is preparing for first solo, the instructor can open the bundle and immediately see the endorsements that usually matter together. That makes it easier to review the training path, catch missing pieces before the student gets sent to the airplane alone, and explain to the student why solo authorization is a process instead of a single signature.

Endorsements in the first-solo bundle

A.3

Pre-solo aeronautical knowledge

Used before first solo to confirm the student passed the required pre-solo knowledge test for that make and model.

14 CFR § 61.87(b)

A.4

Pre-solo flight training

Shows the student received the required pre-solo flight training and is proficient in that make and model.

14 CFR § 61.87(c)(1) and (2)

A.6

Solo flight (first 90-calendar-day period)

Authorizes the student's first solo flights in a specific make and model. It expires in 90 calendar days.

14 CFR § 61.87(n)

A.14

TSA citizenship endorsement

Records the TSA-recommended citizenship endorsement when the instructor uses the endorsement method instead of keeping document copies.

49 CFR § 1552.15(c)

FAQ: first solo endorsements

Which endorsements usually matter for first solo?
In the common first-solo airplane path, instructors usually work through A.3 for pre-solo aeronautical knowledge, A.4 for pre-solo flight training, and A.6 for the initial 90-day solo authorization. A.14 is the common supplemental item when the instructor uses the TSA citizenship endorsement method.
Does the first solo endorsement expire?
Yes. A.6, the initial solo flight endorsement, expires after 90 calendar days. The knowledge and training endorsements are not the same thing as the solo authorization window.
Is the TSA citizenship endorsement always required in the logbook?
Not always. A.14 is the TSA-recommended logbook endorsement when the instructor uses the endorsement method instead of keeping copies of the student's citizenship documents. Instructors still need to follow the actual TSA documentation rules that apply.
Does night solo use the same endorsements?
No. Night solo has a separate endorsement path. The first-solo bundle is for the common initial daytime solo flow, while night solo adds different training and authorization requirements.
