How to Get an FCC Restricted Radiotelephone Operator Permit

Learn when pilots may need an FCC Restricted Radiotelephone Operator Permit, what it is for, and how to plan the application.

Diego Suarez - Updated 2026-04-27 - 3 min read
On this page When Pilots May Need One Basic Eligibility The Application Flow Do Not Confuse FAA and FCC Numbers

Most pilots flying only inside the United States do not think much about a radio operator permit. Normal aviation radio work is part of pilot training and checkrides. But if you plan to fly internationally, an FCC Restricted Radiotelephone Operator Permit may become part of your paperwork checklist.

This permit is commonly referred to as an RP. It authorizes the holder to operate certain aircraft radio stations and is often discussed when pilots plan border-crossing flights.

When Pilots May Need One

For many domestic U.S. general aviation flights, a separate radio operator permit is not normally a practical concern. International flying is different. Other countries may expect pilots to hold radiotelephone authorization, and aircraft station licensing requirements may also apply depending on the operation.

If you plan to fly to Canada, Mexico, the Bahamas, or another international destination, check FCC, FAA, and destination-country requirements before the trip. Do this early, not the week you plan to depart.

Airline and professional pilots often obtain the permit because international operations are common or possible.

For the FAA-side identifier used in testing and certification, review how to get an FAA Tracking Number. For broader international training and certificate paperwork, see foreign pilot license conversion.

Basic Eligibility

The FCC permit is generally issued without an exam for eligible applicants. Typical eligibility expectations include being legally qualified under the applicable FCC rules, being able to communicate, keeping required records when needed, and understanding the rules that govern the radio station you will operate.

The exact application language and fee structure can change, so use the FCC online system and instructions when applying.

The Application Flow

The process generally starts by creating or using an FCC account and obtaining an FCC Registration Number, known as an FRN. This is separate from an FAA Tracking Number.

After that, you use the FCC licensing system to apply for the appropriate restricted radiotelephone operator authorization. You will provide personal information, answer eligibility questions, certify the application, and pay any required fee.

Once processed, the permit is available electronically. Keep a copy with your aviation documents if you need it for international operations.

Do Not Confuse FAA and FCC Numbers

Pilots deal with a lot of identifiers:

  • FAA certificate number.
  • FAA Tracking Number.
  • FCC Registration Number.
  • Aircraft registration number.
  • Radio station authorization if applicable.

They are not interchangeable. The FTN is used in FAA airman certification and testing. The FRN is used by the FCC. If a form asks for one, do not enter the other.

Build an International Flight Paperwork Folder

The RP is only one piece of international planning. Depending on the flight, you may also need to review:

  • Pilot certificate and medical documents.
  • Passport.
  • Aircraft registration and airworthiness documents.
  • Insurance requirements.
  • Customs and border procedures.
  • eAPIS or other arrival/departure filings.
  • Aircraft radio station authorization.
  • Survival equipment requirements.
  • Destination-country operating rules.

International flights are manageable, but they are paperwork-heavy. A simple folder and checklist can prevent last-minute stress.

Final Takeaway

The Restricted Radiotelephone Operator Permit is not difficult for many pilots to obtain, but it is easy to forget until an international trip is close. If cross-border flying is in your future, handle it early and verify the FCC process before applying.
