Flight Computers for Pilots: E6B, App, Electronic

Compare mechanical E6B, electronic flight computers, and app-based flight computers for pilot training, FAA tests, planning, and cockpit use.

Diego Suarez - Updated 2026-04-28 - 4 min read
On this page Mechanical E6B Electronic Flight Computers App-Based Flight Computers Which One Should a Student Buy?

A flight computer helps pilots solve aviation math: time, speed, distance, fuel, wind correction, groundspeed, density altitude, and unit conversions.

Modern avionics and apps can do much of this quickly, but student pilots still benefit from understanding the math behind the answer.

Mechanical E6B

The mechanical E6B, often called a whiz wheel, is the classic flight computer. It needs no batteries, works offline, and teaches the relationships behind the calculations.

It can solve basic multiplication and division, time-speed-distance, fuel burn, true airspeed, density altitude, wind correction, and groundspeed.

The downside is speed and learning curve. You must understand the scales, decimal placement, and wind side setup. Early on, it feels awkward. With practice, it becomes a useful backup.

For FAA knowledge tests, mechanical flight computers are generally the safest choice because they do not store data and are widely accepted. Always check current testing rules before exam day.

Electronic Flight Computers

Electronic flight computers do many of the same calculations as a mechanical E6B but with menus, buttons, and a screen.

They are faster and easier for many students once the workflow is learned. They may include timers, unit conversions, weight and balance tools, and wind calculations.

The tradeoff is power and approval. Not every electronic calculator is allowed for every test environment. If you plan to use one for an FAA knowledge test, verify that it is permitted and that memory can be cleared as required.

App-Based Flight Computers

App-based flight computers and EFB tools are extremely convenient for planning. They can combine weather, aircraft profiles, winds aloft, route planning, fuel, and performance in one place.

They are excellent for everyday flying, but they are not a substitute for understanding. Tablets overheat, batteries die, databases expire, and fingers make bad taps in turbulence.

Apps are usually not allowed for FAA knowledge testing, so student pilots still need a test-acceptable tool.

Which One Should a Student Buy?

Most student pilots should learn a mechanical E6B first or at least understand one. It builds strong mental models and gives you a battery-free backup.

If your budget allows, an approved electronic flight computer can reduce test and planning workload. If you already fly with an EFB, the app tools can be great for real-world planning.

The strongest setup is often:

  • Mechanical E6B for learning and backup.
  • Approved electronic computer if you prefer faster test calculations.
  • EFB app for real-world planning after you understand the inputs.

What to Practice

Do not practice only easy problems. Work through:

  • Wind correction angle.
  • Groundspeed.
  • Fuel required.
  • Estimated time en route.
  • Density altitude.
  • Pressure altitude.
  • Unit conversions.
  • Crosswind component.

Then compare answers between tools. If the app, E6B, and mental estimate disagree wildly, stop and find the input error.

Buying Tips

Choose a tool you will actually use. A beautiful metal E6B is useless if it stays in the bag. A powerful app is dangerous if you enter the wrong aircraft profile. An electronic computer is not helpful if you first open it during the exam.

Also consider readability. Small markings, dim screens, and confusing menus all become harder under pressure.

How to Build Confidence

Use one problem and solve it three ways. Estimate the answer mentally, calculate it with your E6B or electronic computer, and then compare it with an app or planning tool when appropriate. If all three are close, you are probably thinking correctly. If one answer is far away, the issue is usually a unit, decimal, wind direction, or data-entry error.

This habit matters because aviation math is easy to make look precise while still being wrong. A calculator can give a clean number from bad inputs. A pilot needs to notice when the result does not make sense.

For example, if a short leg suddenly shows a fuel burn that seems impossible, pause. Check whether you entered gallons per hour, total fuel, nautical miles, statute miles, true course, or wind direction correctly.

Exam and Cockpit Use Are Different

For testing, choose a tool that meets the current rules and that you can use quickly under pressure. For flying, choose tools that fit your cockpit workflow and backup plan. Those may be the same tool, but they do not have to be.

Train with the tool you plan to use. Familiarity is part of safety.

Student-Pilot Takeaway

The best flight computer is not the one that does the most. It is the one that helps you understand and verify the answer.

Use technology, but keep your pilot brain involved. The calculation is only useful if the inputs make sense.
