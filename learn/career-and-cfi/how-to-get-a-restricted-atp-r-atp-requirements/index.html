How to Get a Restricted ATP: R-ATP Requirements Explained

Learn what a restricted ATP is, who may qualify, how R-ATP hour reductions work, and what pilots should verify before planning an airline path.

Diego Suarez - Updated 2026-04-27 - 4 min read
On this page What the R-ATP Is Common Eligibility Categories Hour Reductions ATP CTP Comes Before the Knowledge Test

A restricted ATP, usually called an R-ATP, is a version of the airline transport pilot certificate that allows eligible pilots to serve as second in command in qualifying airline operations before meeting every requirement for an unrestricted ATP.

For a student pilot or commercial pilot planning an airline career, the R-ATP matters because it can affect the path to airline second-in-command eligibility. But it is not a shortcut around training quality. It is a specific FAA pathway with specific eligibility rules.

What the R-ATP Is

The full ATP certificate is the top pilot certificate in the FAA system. Airline pilots operating in Part 121 service generally need ATP-level qualification. For the broader certificate path, see How to Get an Airline Transport Pilot Certificate.

The R-ATP was created for pilots with certain types of approved training or military experience. It recognizes that some structured training paths may qualify pilots for airline second-in-command privileges with fewer total hours than the standard unrestricted ATP path.

The key limitation is in the name: restricted. A pilot with an R-ATP does not have every unrestricted ATP privilege. The certificate is mainly useful for qualifying to serve as a first officer while continuing to build experience toward full ATP privileges.

Common Eligibility Categories

R-ATP eligibility can depend on your background. Common categories include:

  • U.S. military pilot experience.
  • Graduation from certain approved aviation degree programs.
  • Completion of qualifying aviation coursework and associated flight training.
  • Meeting the standard hour path while using the reduced cross-country requirement available for restricted ATP eligibility.

The details matter. For college-based reductions, the degree, aviation coursework, school authorization, and where the flight training was completed can all affect eligibility. Do not assume that any aviation degree automatically qualifies.

Hour Reductions

The standard unrestricted ATP path is commonly associated with 1,500 hours total time. Some R-ATP pathways may reduce the total flight time requirement for eligible applicants.

For example, military pilots and graduates of approved aviation programs may qualify under reduced total-time thresholds. Other pilots may still need 1,500 total hours but may qualify for the restricted certificate with a lower cross-country requirement than the unrestricted ATP.

Because these are regulatory details, verify your exact pathway against applicable FAA requirements and your school’s approved authorization. A small misunderstanding early in training can become expensive later.

ATP CTP Comes Before the Knowledge Test

Before taking the ATP knowledge test, applicants generally must complete the Airline Transport Pilot Certification Training Program, known as ATP CTP.

ATP CTP is designed to bridge the gap between smaller-aircraft training and the airline environment. It covers topics such as high-altitude operations, advanced aerodynamics, weather, air carrier operations, leadership, crew resource management, and simulator training.

Some pilots complete ATP CTP through an airline training program. Others complete it independently or through an approved training provider. You will also need clean testing records, so handle your FAA Tracking Number and certificate paperwork carefully.

Restricted ATP vs. Frozen ATP

Pilots sometimes hear the phrase “frozen ATP.” In U.S. FAA training, that is not the same thing as an R-ATP.

“Frozen ATP” is used in some international systems to describe a pilot who has completed ATP theory requirements but has not yet met experience requirements. The FAA restricted ATP is a specific certificate with defined privileges and eligibility criteria.

If you are training in the United States, use the FAA terminology and confirm requirements under the FAA system.

How to Plan for R-ATP Early

If you are considering an aviation university or military path, ask direct questions before committing:

  • Is the program FAA-approved for R-ATP hour reduction?
  • Which hour reduction applies?
  • Must flight training be completed at the same institution?
  • What paperwork proves eligibility?
  • What happens if you transfer schools?
  • Does the program still fit your budget and goals if airline hiring slows?

Do not choose a program only for a lower hour number. Choose it because the training quality, structure, cost, and career support make sense.

Final Takeaway

The restricted ATP can be a useful airline-career pathway for eligible pilots, but it is paperwork-sensitive and rule-sensitive. Start with the big picture, then verify the details.

A good R-ATP plan is built early: choose the right training path, keep clean records, protect your medical eligibility, and confirm each requirement before you count on the reduction.
