How to Become a Delta Pilot: A Practical Roadmap

Learn a practical Delta pilot roadmap, including FAA certificates, flight time, medical planning, experience building, and employer verification.

Diego Suarez - Updated 2026-04-28 - 4 min read
On this page Start With the FAA Path Build More Than Minimum Hours Understand the ATP Step Company Pathways and Programs

Becoming a Delta pilot is a long-term goal, not a single application step. You need FAA certificates, flight experience, strong judgment, a clean professional record, and the ability to work well in a crew environment.

Airline hiring requirements and pathways change, so treat any company-specific details as something to verify directly with Delta’s current pilot hiring information. The broad path, however, is stable: learn to fly, build experience, earn an ATP, and become competitive for a major airline cockpit.

If you are still early in training, use this as a career map, not as a promise that any specific hiring program, partner school, or interview standard will still exist when you apply.

Start With the FAA Path

Before a major airline can consider you, you need the right pilot qualifications. A typical civilian route looks like this:

  1. Student pilot certificate.
  2. Private pilot certificate.
  3. Instrument rating.
  4. Commercial pilot certificate.
  5. Multi-engine experience.
  6. Flight instructor or other time-building job.
  7. Airline transport pilot certificate or restricted ATP eligibility.

Along the way, you also need the appropriate medical qualification. Airline flying generally requires high medical standards, so if your goal is the airlines, handle medical questions early with qualified aviation medical guidance.

For the broader training sequence, start with how to become a pilot. For the commercial portion of the path, use the commercial pilot timeline.

Build More Than Minimum Hours

Minimum flight time gets you legally eligible. It does not automatically make you competitive.

Major airlines usually want pilots who have seen a wide range of real-world operations: weather, busy airspace, crew coordination, abnormal situations, passengers, dispatch pressure, and professional decision-making.

Common ways to build that experience include:

  • Flight instructing.
  • Regional airline flying.
  • Military flying.
  • Charter or corporate flying.
  • Cargo operations.
  • Other commercial aviation roles.

Flight instructing is popular because it builds hours while sharpening fundamentals. Regional airline flying is common because it develops crew resource management, turbine experience, and airline-style operations. If instructing is part of your plan, see how to become a flight instructor.

Understand the ATP Step

An airline transport pilot certificate is the top FAA pilot certificate and is normally required for airline operations. The unrestricted ATP path commonly includes a minimum age of 23 and substantial flight time. Restricted ATP pathways may apply to certain military pilots and graduates of approved aviation programs.

The ATP is not just an hour-count milestone. It reflects a higher standard of knowledge, judgment, and aircraft command ability. If you want to fly for Delta, think of every earlier certificate as part of your ATP foundation.

Company Pathways and Programs

Large airlines may offer career pathways, university partnerships, employee pathways, or cadet-style programs at different times. Delta's own Propel information, partner lists, and candidate criteria can change, so do not rely on old screenshots, forum posts, or school marketing copy.

Before committing money or relocating for any airline pathway, verify:

  • Eligibility requirements.
  • Whether a job offer is guaranteed or conditional.
  • Training location and timeline.
  • Costs and financing terms.
  • Medical requirements.
  • Background check standards.
  • Required employment steps before reaching mainline flying.

Do not choose a flight school only because a program sounds exciting. Choose a path that still makes sense if hiring slows, timelines change, or your goal shifts.

When you are close to applying, verify Delta's official pilot hiring FAQ for the current age, work authorization, passport, medical, certificate, ATP written, flight time, turbine preference, multi-engine, background check, and degree-preference language.

What Delta and Other Major Airlines Look For

A competitive major-airline pilot is not just a logbook with hours. Airlines care about safety record, professionalism, training history, checkride performance, judgment, and personality.

Crew flying requires humility. You need to speak up when something is wrong and listen when another pilot catches your mistake. You need to brief clearly, follow procedures, and stay calm during abnormal events.

If you are early in training, build those habits now. Show up prepared. Keep clean logbook records. Take checkrides seriously. Avoid careless decisions that could follow you into future interviews.

Interview Preparation

Airline interviews often evaluate technical knowledge, decision-making, communication, and how you work with others. You may be asked about aircraft systems, regulations, weather, emergencies, and past experiences.

Start collecting examples from your flying career:

  • A time you made a conservative safety decision.
  • A time you handled a mistake.
  • A time you worked through conflict.
  • A time you prepared deeply for a difficult flight.
  • A time you learned from a failed or unsatisfactory outcome.

Do not wait until the interview to become reflective. Professional pilots debrief themselves constantly.

Training After a Conditional Offer

If hired, the real work continues. Airline new-hire training can include company indoctrination, systems training, procedures, simulator sessions, checking events, and initial operating experience.

You will be expected to study efficiently, work in a crew, use standard callouts, and fly the airplane the company way. Prior experience helps, but you still need to adapt to the airline’s procedures.

A Practical Roadmap

If Delta is your long-term target, use this roadmap:

  1. Protect your medical eligibility.
  2. Earn certificates cleanly and professionally.
  3. Build strong instrument skills.
  4. Add multi-engine experience.
  5. Choose time-building jobs that develop judgment, not just hours.
  6. Maintain a clean record.
  7. Network respectfully.
  8. Track current airline requirements directly.

The path is long, but it is built one certificate, one logbook entry, and one good decision at a time.
