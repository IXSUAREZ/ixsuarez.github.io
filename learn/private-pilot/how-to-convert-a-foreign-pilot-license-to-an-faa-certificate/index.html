How to Convert a Foreign Pilot License to an FAA Certificate

Learn the basic path to convert a foreign pilot license to an FAA certificate, including verification, medical eligibility, paperwork, TSA checks, and testing.

Diego Suarez - Updated 2026-04-27 - 4 min read

Converting a foreign pilot license to an FAA certificate is possible, but it is a paperwork-heavy process. The exact path depends on your current license, ratings, medical status, citizenship or nationality status, and whether you want a foreign-based FAA certificate or a stand-alone FAA certificate.

Because this is regulatory and time-sensitive, verify current FAA forms, timelines, and requirements before starting.

FAA Certificate vs. Foreign License

The FAA uses the word certificate rather than license. A foreign private pilot license may support issuance of a U.S. private pilot certificate based on that foreign license, if verification is successful.

That kind of certificate is tied to the underlying foreign license. If the foreign license or required medical status is no longer valid, your FAA privileges based on it can be affected.

A stand-alone FAA certificate is different. It requires meeting FAA requirements for the certificate or rating sought, including any required knowledge test and practical test.

Step 1: Verify the Foreign License

The FAA must confirm that your foreign pilot license and ratings are valid. This normally involves submitting a verification request and supporting documents, such as a copy of your foreign pilot license and, when applicable, medical information.

The FAA contacts the civil aviation authority that issued the license. Once verification is complete, you receive a verification letter. Treat that letter as time-limited and follow the window the FAA gives you.

This can take time, so do not book travel or training too tightly around an optimistic date.

Step 2: Prepare for the FAA Meeting

After verification, you generally need to meet with an FAA office representative, designated examiner, or other authorized certifying official to complete the issuance process.

Bring the required identification, foreign pilot license, medical documentation, verification letter, and FAA application materials. Many applications are handled through IACRA.

You must also be able to read, speak, write, and understand English to the required standard.

Foreign-Based FAA Private Pilot Certificate

The foreign-based FAA private pilot certificate can allow certain private pilot privileges in the United States, but it is limited compared with a stand-alone FAA certificate.

If you hold a foreign commercial pilot license, do not assume it converts directly to a stand-alone FAA commercial certificate. A common path is first receiving an FAA private pilot certificate based on the foreign license, then meeting FAA commercial requirements separately.

Instrument Rating Considerations

If your foreign license includes an instrument rating, additional steps may apply before receiving FAA instrument privileges. This can include verification and passing the FAA instrument knowledge test.

Instrument privileges are especially important to clarify because FAA procedures, airspace, phraseology, and approach rules may differ from what you are used to.

Stand-Alone FAA Certificate Path

To earn a stand-alone FAA certificate, expect to meet FAA knowledge, aeronautical experience, training, and skill requirements.

For a private pilot certificate, that typically means preparing for and passing the FAA private pilot knowledge test, then completing a practical test with an oral portion and a flight portion.

For a commercial certificate, the standards are higher and the written and practical testing are more advanced.

Your previous flight experience may help, but the FAA will still expect you to meet U.S. standards. A school that regularly handles conversions can help you identify which experience transfers cleanly and which tasks need U.S.-specific training; start that comparison with how to choose a flight school.

Medical Requirements

You need an appropriate medical path for the privileges you plan to exercise. Depending on the certificate and operation, that may involve an FAA medical certificate or a valid medical associated with the foreign license for certain foreign-based privileges.

If you have medical questions, speak with an Aviation Medical Examiner before you spend money on travel or training.

TSA Training Security Checks

If you are not a U.S. citizen or U.S. national, U.S. flight training may also require TSA Flight Training Security Program review before flight training covered by that program. Do not treat FAA certificate issuance and TSA training eligibility as the same process. Confirm the current TSA process with the flight school before you schedule aircraft time.

Currency After Conversion

After receiving an FAA certificate, you still need to stay current. Private and commercial pilot certificates do not simply expire, but privileges depend on requirements such as flight reviews, medical status, passenger landing currency, and instrument currency if applicable.

For passenger carrying, recent takeoffs and landings matter. For IFR, recent instrument experience matters. For any flight, proficiency matters beyond the minimum legal requirement.

Practical Advice

Start early. Keep copies of every document. Use the same name formatting across licenses, passports, applications, and medical documents when possible. Work with a U.S. flight school or instructor familiar with foreign pilot conversions.

Also budget for transition training. Even experienced pilots need time to adapt to U.S. airspace, radio work, charting, weather products, and checkride expectations. Start with ATC communication if U.S. radio procedures are unfamiliar.

Bottom Line

Converting a foreign pilot license to an FAA certificate is manageable when you break it into steps: verification, paperwork, medical eligibility, FAA meeting, and any required testing.

Treat it as both an administrative process and a flying transition. The paperwork gets you started. The training makes you safe and comfortable in the U.S. system.
