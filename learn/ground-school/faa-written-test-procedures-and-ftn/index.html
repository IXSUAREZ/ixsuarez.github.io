FAA Written Test Procedures and the FTN

Learn what the FAA Tracking Number is, why it matters for FAA written tests, and how student pilots should prepare for knowledge testing.

Diego Suarez - Updated 2026-04-27 - 3 min read
On this page What Is an FTN? Where Do You Get an FTN? Why the FAA Made the Change What About Existing Pilots?

FAA written test procedures changed years ago to make airman certification records more consistent. The key idea for applicants is the FAA Tracking Number, usually called an FTN.

This topic is no longer a future change. For applicants, the useful angle is not "change coming," but "understand why the FTN matters before scheduling a knowledge test."

If you are still early in the process, start with How to Get an FAA Tracking Number and then use Checklist to Ace Your FAA Private Pilot Written Test as a study companion.

What Is an FTN?

An FAA Tracking Number is a unique identifier connected to an airman or applicant in the FAA certification system. It helps the FAA track applications, tests, certificates, and records without relying only on names.

Names can be duplicated, misspelled, changed, or entered inconsistently. A stable number reduces confusion and helps connect records throughout a pilot's aviation career.

Where Do You Get an FTN?

Applicants get an FTN through the FAA's IACRA system. IACRA is the online application system used for many pilot certification tasks.

For a student pilot or remote pilot applicant, the practical flow is usually:

  1. Create or access an IACRA profile.
  2. Receive or locate the FTN.
  3. Use that FTN when scheduling the knowledge test.
  4. Keep the Airman Knowledge Test Report after the exam.

Testing-provider procedures, identification rules, and scheduling screens can change. Verify the current FAA and testing-provider instructions before you book the exam.

Why the FAA Made the Change

The reasons are practical: cleaner recordkeeping, fewer name-matching errors, better digital processing, improved data quality, and a more standardized certification path.

From the applicant side, the change is not difficult. The main mistake is waiting until test day to realize you needed an FTN before scheduling.

What About Existing Pilots?

People who already had FAA certificates may already have an FTN, even if they did not remember creating one. In that case, the pilot can usually locate it through their FAA/IACRA profile.

If you already hold a certificate and are adding a rating or taking another knowledge test, do not create duplicate records casually. Work through the proper account recovery or profile process.

AKTR and Test Codes

After a knowledge test, applicants receive an Airman Knowledge Test Report, or AKTR. This report includes the test result and missed-topic codes.

The FAA shifted from older code styles toward ACS-aligned codes. The useful training point is that missed-topic codes are not just administrative marks. They tell you what to study before the oral exam and checkride.

If you miss a weather question, airspace topic, or performance calculation area, use the code to find the knowledge gap.

Drone and Airplane Applicants

The FTN process applies broadly to FAA certification applicants, including pilots of manned aircraft and drone pilot applicants.

For SuarezCFI.com readers, the relevant lesson is the general FAA testing habit: know the administrative steps before you focus only on study content.

Student Pilot Testing Checklist

Before scheduling a written test:

  • Confirm which knowledge test you need.
  • Create or locate your FAA/IACRA profile.
  • Record your FTN.
  • Verify testing provider requirements.
  • Bring required identification.
  • Keep your AKTR after the test.
  • Review missed-topic codes with your instructor.

Before You Schedule

The FTN made FAA written test records more consistent. For a student pilot, it is simply part of the normal testing workflow. Get your FAA profile organized early so administrative details do not slow down your training.
