TAA vs Complex: Commercial Training Credit

Understand TAA vs complex airplane requirements for commercial pilot training, including definitions, endorsements, logging, and training strategy.

Diego Suarez - Updated 2026-04-27 - 4 min read
On this page The Commercial Training Bucket What Makes an Airplane Complex? What Makes an Airplane TAA? Which One Should You Choose?

Commercial pilot applicants often hear two phrases that sound similar but mean very different things: complex airplane and technically advanced airplane, or TAA. Both can matter when you are planning commercial training, but they build different skills.

A complex airplane teaches mechanical systems: landing gear, propeller control, flap management, and the discipline of verifying configuration. A TAA teaches avionics management: glass displays, GPS navigation, automation, and mode awareness.

For a student trying to control cost and schedule, the practical question is simple: which one counts, and which one should I fly?

The Commercial Training Bucket

Under FAA commercial airplane training rules, applicants need a specific amount of training in a complex airplane, turbine-powered airplane, technically advanced airplane, or a combination of those. Because this is regulatory and can change, verify the current text of 14 CFR Part 61 with your instructor before building a training plan.

The important student-pilot takeaway is that complex time is no longer the only common path. A properly equipped TAA may satisfy the requirement, depending on the exact aircraft and training.

That flexibility helps because many flight schools have more glass-panel fixed-gear airplanes available than retractable-gear trainers. It can also reduce maintenance delays. But flexibility does not mean every panel upgrade qualifies.

What Makes an Airplane Complex?

A complex airplane generally has all three of these:

  • Retractable landing gear
  • Flaps
  • A controllable-pitch propeller

A Cessna 172 with flaps is not complex. It lacks retractable gear and a controllable-pitch propeller. A Piper Arrow is a common training example of a complex airplane because it has the required systems.

To act as pilot in command of a complex airplane, most pilots need ground and flight training plus a one-time logbook endorsement from an authorized instructor. That endorsement is not just paperwork. You should understand gear operation, propeller operation, emergency extension, performance changes, and configuration discipline.

What Makes an Airplane TAA?

A technically advanced airplane is defined by installed avionics and automation. A TAA generally needs an electronic primary flight display, an electronic multifunction display with GPS moving map capability, and an integrated two-axis autopilot.

An iPad and a basic electronic instrument do not automatically make an airplane a TAA. The required equipment must be installed and available in a way that meets the FAA definition.

TAA flying is valuable because modern cockpit work is not just stick-and-rudder. You need to manage navigation sources, autopilot modes, display failures, alerts, and automation surprises while still flying the airplane.

Which One Should You Choose?

If your school has a reliable complex airplane, some complex time is still valuable. You learn habits that transfer to many aircraft: gear checks, propeller settings, configuration callouts, and system failure thinking.

If complex aircraft are expensive, unavailable, or frequently down for maintenance, TAA time may be a practical way to keep training moving. It also prepares you for the automation-heavy cockpits used in much of modern aviation.

A hybrid approach can work well. For example, a student might complete part of the requirement in a TAA and still get several hours in a complex airplane for systems exposure. That plan can balance cost, scheduling, and skill development.

Logging Matters

Do not leave the examiner guessing. Your logbook entries should clearly show the aircraft used, dual instruction received, and why the flight counts. If the aircraft is a TAA, the entry should make that obvious. If it is complex, the endorsement and training should be documented.

Good logging is boring, clear, and specific. Ask your instructor how they want each commercial training flight recorded so the entries will be easy to review during checkride eligibility.

Checkride Preparation

Even if you complete the requirement in a TAA, you should still understand complex systems at a practical level. If you complete it in a complex airplane, you should still be comfortable with modern avionics and automation.

For TAA-heavy training, be ready to discuss autopilot modes, GPS navigation, display failure, reversionary mode if applicable, and how you maintain aircraft control when automation is disconnected.

For complex-heavy training, be ready for questions about gear extension, propeller governing, flap limitations, emergency procedures, and how to prevent gear-up landing errors.

The Best Choice Is the One You Can Train Well In

Do not choose an aircraft only because it looks better in a logbook. Choose the aircraft that lets you train consistently, meet the requirement legally, and build the skills you actually need.

TAA and complex airplanes are not rivals. They teach different parts of being a commercial pilot. The strongest path is the one that gives you legal credit, practical proficiency, and fewer surprises on checkride day.
