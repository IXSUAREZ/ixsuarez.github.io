How to Find FAA Testing Centers for Any Aviation Exam

Learn how to find FAA testing centers, get an FAA Tracking Number, schedule with PSI, prepare test-day documents, and avoid common test-day problems.

Diego Suarez - Updated 2026-04-28 - 4 min read
On this page Step 1: Get Your FAA Tracking Number Step 2: Use the PSI Test Center Search Step 3: Pick the Correct Exam Step 4: Know What You Need to Bring

FAA airman knowledge tests are scheduled through the FAA's authorized testing system and linked scheduling provider. Whether you are taking a private pilot, instrument, commercial, flight instructor, aircraft dispatcher, or remote pilot test, the basic workflow is similar: get your FAA Tracking Number, create the testing account, choose the correct exam, and schedule a seat at an authorized testing center.

The process is not hard, but the details matter. A wrong test code, mismatched name, or missing authorization can cost you time and money.

Step 1: Get Your FAA Tracking Number

Your FAA Tracking Number, commonly called an FTN, is your unique identifier in the FAA certification system. You get it by creating an applicant account in IACRA, the FAA's certification application system. If you need the full walkthrough, start with how to get an FAA Tracking Number.

Use your legal name exactly as it appears on your identification. If your name does not match across IACRA, PSI, your endorsement, and your ID, you may have problems on test day.

Once your FTN is issued, keep it somewhere easy to find. You will use it throughout your aviation training and certification path.

Step 2: Use the PSI Test Center Search

After you have an FTN, use the FAA airman testing page and the linked scheduling site to search for authorized testing centers. Center availability, appointment times, and fees can change, so use the scheduler as the controlling source before you make plans around a specific location.

Look closely at each location. Some test centers may have better parking, more appointment times, easier access, or clearer check-in procedures. If you have multiple options, choose the site that gives you the least stress on exam day.

Step 3: Pick the Correct Exam

FAA exam names can look similar. "Private Pilot" can include different aircraft categories. Instrument, commercial, instructor, and remote pilot exams each have their own test codes and requirements.

Before paying, verify:

  • The exact certificate or rating.
  • The aircraft category, if applicable.
  • The correct test code.
  • The authorization requirement.
  • The number of questions and time limit.
  • The required passing score.

If you are training with an instructor, confirm the exam selection before you schedule.

Step 4: Know What You Need to Bring

At minimum, expect to bring valid identification that matches your application information. Many FAA tests also require proof of authorization, such as a graduation certificate, logbook endorsement, or written statement from an instructor.

The exact documents depend on the exam. Check the FAA knowledge test matrix and the appointment instructions carefully.

You may also be allowed to bring certain tools such as an E6B, plotter, or non-programmable calculator for pilot exams. Testing centers usually restrict phones, notes, bags, food, smartwatches, and other personal items in the room.

Step 5: Budget for the Fee

FAA knowledge test fees can vary by exam and testing center. Many common aviation exams fall in a similar price range, but prices can change. Budget for the first attempt and avoid treating a retake as free practice.

Some military applicants may have different reimbursement or testing options depending on their status and program eligibility. Confirm benefits through the proper program before scheduling.

Avoid the Common Scheduling Traps

The most common mistake is scheduling before you are truly eligible. Some exams need an instructor endorsement, graduation certificate, or other authorization. If the testing center cannot verify your eligibility, you may not be allowed to test.

Another mistake is using a nickname or shortened name on one account and a legal name on another. Keep the same name format everywhere.

Finally, do not wait until the last possible week. Testing centers can fill up, especially near holidays, school breaks, and common checkride seasons. If your written test is tied to a training deadline, schedule with enough margin to handle a canceled appointment or illness.

If You Fail

Failing is frustrating, but it is not the end of training. You will receive a test report showing areas that need work. For many FAA airman knowledge tests, an additional instructor endorsement or other authorization step may be required before retesting, so check the test rules for your specific exam.

Use the report to study targeted weak areas instead of simply rereading everything. The retake costs money, so make the next attempt deliberate.

Rescheduling

If weather, illness, or life gets in the way, reschedule early. Testing vendors normally have deadlines for canceling or rebooking without losing the fee. Do not wait until the last minute unless it is unavoidable.

The best testing day is boring: your documents match, your test code is correct, your calculator is allowed, and you arrive early. Handle the admin cleanly so your attention can stay on the exam.

If the test is part of a certificate path, keep the written test timing connected to the rest of training. The step-by-step guides for the private pilot certificate and commercial pilot certificate show where the knowledge test fits in the larger sequence.
