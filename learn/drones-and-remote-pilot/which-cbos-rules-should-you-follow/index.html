Which CBO Rules Should You Follow?

Learn how recreational drone pilots should think about CBO safety guidelines, FAA-recognized organizations, FPV, night flying, and documentation.

Recreational drone pilots do not operate in a rule-free space. In the United States, recreational flying under the limited recreational exception includes following safety guidelines from an FAA-recognized community-based organization, usually called a CBO.

That raises a practical question: which CBO rules should you follow?

The short answer is that you should choose safety guidelines that match the kind of recreational flying you plan to do, understand them before launch, and be able to explain what you are following if asked.

What a CBO Does

A community-based organization provides safety guidelines for recreational model aircraft and drone operations. These guidelines fill in practical details for normal hobby flying, FPV operations, night operations, spectator separation, field procedures, and other situations.

The FAA recognizes CBOs, and recreational flyers can use their published safety guidelines as part of operating under the recreational exception.

CBO guidance does not erase the rest of the rules. You still need to follow airspace requirements, registration requirements, TRUST, visual line of sight, altitude limits, and any other applicable FAA rules.

Do You Need to Be a Member?

Membership and rule use are separate ideas.

You may be able to follow a CBO's published safety guidelines without being a member, depending on the organization and the operation. Some specialized activities, club fields, insurance programs, or events may have membership requirements, so read the actual guidelines and field rules before assuming.

For normal recreational planning, the key is not collecting logos from organizations. The key is knowing which safety guidelines apply to your flight.

Can You Switch Between CBO Guidelines?

You do not have to treat one CBO as your permanent rulebook for every recreational flight. Different organizations may have guidance that fits different types of flying.

For example:

  • One set of guidelines may be useful for traditional model aircraft.
  • Another may address FPV flying more directly.
  • Another may have clearer night flying procedures.
  • A local club may have field-specific rules on top of broader guidance.

The important part is consistency for the flight you are conducting. Do not mix rules casually in a way that creates confusion. Decide what you are following before takeoff.

What the Guidelines Often Cover

Most CBO safety guidelines overlap on the basics:

  • Do not fly carelessly or recklessly.
  • Yield to crewed aircraft.
  • Maintain visual line of sight.
  • Use a visual observer for FPV when required.
  • Keep observers close enough for direct communication.
  • Do not fly over people unless allowed by the applicable rules.
  • Check airspace before flying.
  • Avoid temporary flight restrictions.
  • Inspect the aircraft before flight.
  • Use good judgment with weather, fatigue, and distractions.

Some differences show up in spectator distances, night lighting, FPV procedures, field etiquette, and special aircraft types.

FPV Flying

FPV is where CBO details matter a lot. Flying through goggles can reduce the pilot's direct awareness of the aircraft and surrounding airspace, so visual observer procedures are important.

A good FPV plan should answer:

  • Who is the visual observer?
  • Can the observer see the drone?
  • Can the observer talk directly to the pilot?
  • What happens if the observer loses sight?
  • Are there people, roads, buildings, or aircraft nearby?

If the plan depends on vague communication or someone casually watching, improve the plan before flying.

Night Operations

Night recreational flying requires extra caution. Some CBO guidelines include lighting requirements, training recommendations, or limits for night operations.

Small drones are easy to lose at night. Lights help, but they can also affect weight and registration status. If a light pushes a drone over 250 grams, that can create additional requirements, including Remote ID planning when registration is required.

Before night flight, confirm the aircraft can be seen, orientation can be understood, and the operation follows current rules and the chosen CBO guidelines.

Keep Proof Handy

If someone with authority asks what safety guidelines you are following, you should not be guessing from memory.

Keep a digital copy or printed copy of the relevant guidelines. Also keep proof of TRUST completion, registration if required, and airspace authorization if applicable.

This is basic pilot professionalism. It also makes your own planning better.

The Takeaway

Choose CBO safety guidelines that fit the recreational flight you are actually planning. Read them before takeoff, follow them consistently, and keep documentation available.

Recreational drone flying stays simpler when you treat it like aviation: plan first, fly within the rules, and leave yourself a margin.
