How to Get an Aircraft Dispatcher Job

Learn how to become an aircraft dispatcher, including FAA eligibility, approved training paths, ADX testing, experience building, and job search basics.

Diego Suarez - Updated 2026-04-27 - 3 min read
On this page What Dispatchers Do Basic FAA Eligibility Training The ADX Knowledge Test

Aircraft dispatchers help plan and monitor airline flights from the ground. They review weather, performance, routing, fuel, airport conditions, regulations, and operational risks. In airline operations, dispatchers share responsibility for safe flight release with the pilot in command.

If you like aviation but do not necessarily want to fly for a living, dispatch can be a serious career path.

What Dispatchers Do

A dispatcher’s workday may include:

  • Reviewing METARs, TAFs, radar, NOTAMs, and operational messages.
  • Building or reviewing flight plans.
  • Evaluating fuel requirements.
  • Considering alternate airports.
  • Coordinating with pilots and operations teams.
  • Monitoring flights after departure.
  • Helping respond to delays, diversions, weather, or mechanical issues.

The job requires calm decision-making and clear communication. Dispatchers work behind the scenes, but their decisions affect real flights.

Basic FAA Eligibility

To earn an FAA aircraft dispatcher certificate, applicants generally must meet age, English language, training, and testing requirements. Under FAA rules, a person may be eligible to take the dispatcher knowledge test at 21, but certificate eligibility requires being at least 23. A high school diploma or equivalent may be expected by training programs or employers.

Dispatcher certification is governed by FAA rules, so verify the applicable requirements before enrolling in a program. Training programs should be FAA-approved if you plan to use them for certificate eligibility.

Training

Aircraft dispatcher training covers the knowledge needed to plan and release flights safely. Expect topics such as:

  • Regulations.
  • Weather theory and reports.
  • Navigation.
  • Aircraft performance.
  • Weight and balance.
  • Flight planning.
  • Air traffic control procedures.
  • Crew coordination.
  • Emergency and abnormal operations.

Some programs are full-time and intensive. Others are part-time or hybrid. Choose a program based on approval status, schedule, quality of instruction, graduate support, and realistic total cost.

Do not choose only by speed. A short program still needs enough structure for you to actually learn the material.

The ADX Knowledge Test

The aircraft dispatcher knowledge test evaluates aviation knowledge at a professional level. It is not just a memorization exercise. You need to understand weather, regulations, flight planning, aircraft performance, and operational judgment.

Before scheduling, you will need the required FAA testing setup, including an FAA Tracking Number. Testing provider procedures can change, so use FAA and testing-center instructions when you schedule.

The Practical Test

After meeting training and knowledge-test requirements, applicants complete a practical test with an FAA inspector or designated aircraft dispatcher examiner.

The practical test commonly includes an oral portion and scenario-based flight planning. You may need to explain weather decisions, fuel planning, alternates, route choices, regulatory compliance, and operational risk.

The examiner wants to see that you can think like a dispatcher, not just repeat definitions.

Building Experience

New dispatchers may start in entry-level operations roles before moving into full dispatcher positions. Titles and duties vary, but useful experience can include operations control, crew scheduling support, ramp or station operations, flight following, and dispatch assistant work.

Internships can also help. The goal is to learn how airline operations work in real time: delays, maintenance, weather, crew legality, airport constraints, and communication pressure.

Applying for Jobs

When applying, make your resume specific. Highlight the certificate, training program, weather knowledge, flight planning tools, operations experience, communication skill, and any high-pressure decision-making background.

Prepare for scenario questions in interviews. Employers may ask how you would respond to a weather change, aircraft limitation, fuel concern, or diversion decision. Talk through the reasoning calmly.

Be open to relocation. Aircraft dispatcher jobs are tied to operation centers, airlines, cargo operators, and aviation companies. Your first job may not be in your preferred city.

Final Takeaway

Aircraft dispatch is a professional aviation role with real safety responsibility. The path is straightforward: meet eligibility requirements, complete approved training, pass the tests, build operational experience, and apply strategically. If you are comparing dispatch with a cockpit career path, the airline pilot certificate ladder is covered in How to Get an Airline Transport Pilot Certificate.

If you enjoy weather, planning, regulations, and making decisions under pressure, dispatch may fit you well.
