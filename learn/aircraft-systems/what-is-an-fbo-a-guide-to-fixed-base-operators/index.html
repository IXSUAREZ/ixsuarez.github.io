What Is an FBO? A Guide to Fixed-Base Operators

Learn what an FBO is, what services fixed-base operators provide, how pilots use them, and what student pilots should ask before arriving.

Diego Suarez - Updated 2026-04-28 - 4 min read
On this page What Services Does an FBO Provide? Why Student Pilots Should Care Fees and Costs Arriving at an FBO

An FBO, or fixed-base operator, is a business or airport-operated facility that provides services to aircraft, pilots, and passengers at an airport. If you fly general aviation, the FBO is often where you park, fuel, pay fees, meet passengers, check weather, grab a crew car, or ask for local airport help.

For a student pilot, the FBO can feel like a private aviation lobby. It may be a quiet counter at a small airport or a large ramp operation at a busy field. The size changes, but the job is similar: support aircraft on the ground.

What Services Does an FBO Provide?

The most common FBO service is fuel. Many sell avgas for piston aircraft and Jet A for turbine aircraft. Some airports have self-serve fuel pumps, while others use fuel trucks and line personnel.

FBOs may also provide aircraft parking, tiedowns, hangar space, lavatory service, deicing, towing, oil, oxygen, basic maintenance coordination, and aircraft cleaning. At larger airports, line staff may marshal aircraft, set chocks, connect ground power, or move aircraft with tugs.

Inside the building, pilots may find restrooms, briefing areas, Wi-Fi, vending or snacks, conference rooms, showers, pilot lounges, and rental car coordination. Some FBOs help arrange hotels, catering, transportation, or customs coordination when the airport supports it.

Not every airport has all of this. A small field may only have a self-serve pump, a code-locked restroom, and a phone number on the door. That can still be enough if you planned for it.

Why Student Pilots Should Care

FBOs are part of real cross-country flying. The checkride may test your flight planning, but day-to-day flying also tests your ability to arrive somewhere, park legally, fuel correctly, and leave without confusion.

Before your first few cross-country flights, practice calling the FBO. Ask simple questions:

  • Is transient parking available?
  • Are there ramp, handling, overnight, or facility fees?
  • Is any fee waived with a fuel purchase?
  • Is self-serve or full-service fuel available?
  • What are the operating hours?
  • Is there a call-out fee after hours?
  • Where should a training aircraft park?

Those questions are not awkward. They are normal pilot questions, and they can prevent an expensive or frustrating surprise.

Fees and Costs

FBO fees vary widely. Airport size, aircraft size, fuel purchase, location, season, and special events can all change the bill. A small piston single may have a very different fee structure than a business jet.

Common charges include ramp fees, handling fees, overnight parking, hangar fees, call-out fees, and fuel minimums. Some FBOs waive certain fees if you buy a required amount of fuel. Others do not.

Because prices and policies change, do not rely on old forum posts or memory. Call ahead, check airport information close to departure, and write down the name of the person you spoke with if the cost matters to the flight.

Arriving at an FBO

Before landing, know which ramp you are going to. At towered airports, ground control may ask where you are parking. At non-towered airports, you still need to taxi safely and avoid wandering onto the wrong ramp.

After landing, follow airport signs, taxi diagrams, ground instructions when applicable, and any line personnel signals. If a marshaller is present, follow standard marshalling signals only when you understand them. If you are unsure, stop the aircraft safely and ask on the radio or by phone.

Once parked, set the brake as appropriate, shut down, secure the aircraft, install chocks or tiedowns if needed, and confirm fuel orders clearly. Be specific: fuel type, quantity, tabs or top-off, which tanks, and whether you want fuel before departure or immediately.

Departing From an FBO

Before you leave, review the invoice, confirm fuel caps are secure, sump as appropriate, check oil, remove chocks and tiedowns, and look around the aircraft for cones, carts, tugs, fuel hoses, or other ramp hazards.

If the FBO moved the aircraft, give yourself time for a complete preflight. Do not let a nice lobby or a helpful line crew rush your normal procedures.

Regulatory and Safety Notes

FBO operations are shaped by airport rules, leases, minimum standards, safety programs, and FAA guidance. Pilots do not need to memorize every business rule, but they do need to respect airport procedures and ramp safety.

Fueling deserves special attention. Static electricity, bonding practices, fuel grade errors, caps left loose, and fuel contamination can all become serious. Stay involved enough to know what was put in your airplane and where.

The Practical Takeaway

An FBO is not just a lobby. It is part of the airport operating environment. Treat it like any other aviation service: plan ahead, ask clear questions, verify fuel and fees, and keep your pilot habits sharp on the ramp.

Good FBO use makes cross-country flying smoother. Poor planning can turn a simple stop into a delay, a surprise bill, or a safety issue.
