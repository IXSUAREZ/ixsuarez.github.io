How to Get a Commercial Pilot Certificate Step by Step

Learn the path to a commercial pilot certificate, including eligibility, medical requirements, training, tests, privileges, and cost planning.

Diego Suarez - Updated 2026-04-28 - 4 min read
On this page Step 1: Meet the Basic Eligibility Requirements Step 2: Handle the Medical Step 3: Build the Required Flight Time Step 4: Complete Ground Training

A commercial pilot certificate is the point where flying can become paid work. It does not automatically make you an airline pilot, and it does not let you do every aviation job by itself. But it is a major step toward professional flying.

The commercial certificate proves that you can fly to a higher standard than a private pilot. The maneuvers are more precise, the judgment expectations are higher, and the privileges come with real limitations.

If you are comparing training structures, read Part 61 vs. Part 141 before you build a budget. The rule path affects minimum hours, school structure, and how rigid the syllabus may be.

Step 1: Meet the Basic Eligibility Requirements

For an airplane commercial pilot certificate, you generally need to be at least 18 years old, able to read, speak, write, and understand English, and already hold at least a private pilot certificate.

You also need the required training, aeronautical experience, endorsements, knowledge test, and practical test.

Step 2: Handle the Medical

Commercial privileges normally require at least a second-class medical certificate when exercising those privileges. You can train with different medical timing depending on your situation, but do not ignore medical eligibility.

If aviation is your career goal, address medical questions early. It is better to understand a medical issue before you spend heavily on advanced training.

Step 3: Build the Required Flight Time

The required total time depends on the training path. Part 61 and Part 141 programs have different structures and minimums. Many pilots working under Part 61 need at least 250 hours total time for the commercial airplane certificate, while approved Part 141 programs may use a lower minimum.

Those hours must include specific types of experience, such as cross-country, instrument, night, solo or performing duties as pilot in command, and checkride preparation items.

Do not think of the hours as empty time building. Use them to become sharper: better weather decisions, better landings, better radio work, better navigation, and better energy management.

Step 4: Complete Ground Training

Commercial ground training expands beyond private pilot knowledge. Expect deeper work in regulations, performance, weather, systems, aerodynamics, navigation, high-altitude considerations, and commercial privileges and limitations.

You must be ready for the knowledge test and for an oral exam where the examiner expects professional-level understanding.

Step 5: Learn the Commercial Maneuvers

Commercial flight training includes maneuvers such as chandelles, lazy eights, steep spirals, eights on pylons, and power-off accuracy approaches, depending on the aircraft category and test requirements.

These are not random tricks. They teach coordination, planning, energy control, and precision. A commercial pilot should look smooth and ahead of the airplane.

For a maneuver-level overview, use commercial maneuvers explained. For a dedicated lazy-eight walkthrough, see how to fly lazy eights smoothly.

Step 6: Understand Complex, TAA, and Aircraft Requirements

Commercial training may require experience in a complex airplane, turbine-powered airplane, or technically advanced airplane depending on the rule set and aircraft used. Do not assume any airplane qualifies just because it is available at the school.

Ask your instructor which aircraft will be used for the required training and the checkride. The avionics, landing gear, flaps, engine controls, and performance should all be familiar before test day.

Step 7: Pass the Knowledge Test

The commercial pilot knowledge test is a required step. It has a validity period, so plan timing with your instructor. Passing early can reduce pressure later, but do not take it before you are prepared.

Step 8: Pass the Checkride

The practical test includes an oral portion and a flight portion. The examiner evaluates whether you meet the applicable standards for knowledge, risk management, and skill.

If you pass, you receive temporary privileges while the permanent certificate is processed.

Privileges and Limits

A commercial certificate allows you to be paid for certain flying, but many operations also require specific rules, certificates, aircraft qualifications, company training, or additional ratings.

Without an instrument rating, commercial airplane privileges may be limited for carrying passengers for hire at night or beyond certain cross-country distances. For most career paths, the instrument rating is not optional in practice.

Cost Planning

Commercial training cost depends mostly on how much time you already have and what aircraft you need. If you are close to the required total time, the final course may be relatively short. If you need many more hours, the budget grows quickly.

Plan for aircraft rental, instructor time, ground study, test fees, checkride fees, and any advanced aircraft checkout required by the program.

The commercial certificate opens doors. Professional judgment is what keeps them open.
