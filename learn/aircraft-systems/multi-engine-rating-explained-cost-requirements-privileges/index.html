Multi-Engine Rating: Cost and Requirements

Learn what a multi-engine rating is, who should get one, what training covers, and how requirements, privileges, and costs work.

Diego Suarez - Updated 2026-04-28 - 3 min read
On this page Why Get a Multi-Engine Rating? How the Rating Is Added Private or Commercial Level? Instrument Considerations

A multi-engine rating lets a pilot fly airplanes with more than one engine. For some private pilots, it is a step into faster, more capable personal aircraft. For career pilots, it is usually an important milestone because many professional flying jobs involve multi-engine airplanes.

The rating is commonly added to an existing private or commercial pilot certificate. If your certificate says airplane single-engine land, the multi-engine rating adds the multi-engine land class when completed in a land airplane.

For the broader decision, compare this with single-engine vs. multi-engine flying. The second engine adds capability, but it also adds training, systems knowledge, and decision pressure.

Why Get a Multi-Engine Rating?

Multi-engine airplanes can offer more speed, payload, range, and systems capability than many single-engine trainers. They also give pilots more options after an engine failure, but only if the pilot is trained and proficient.

That last part matters. A twin is not automatically safer just because it has two engines. Engine-out control, decision-making, and performance knowledge are central to multi-engine flying.

If you want an airline, cargo, corporate, charter, or advanced instruction path, multi-engine experience becomes especially important.

How the Rating Is Added

For many pilots, the multi-engine rating is an additional class rating. There is typically no separate FAA knowledge test just to add the rating, but the practical test includes an oral portion and a flight portion.

Your instructor must train and endorse you when you are ready. The checkride focuses on multi-engine systems, performance, V-speeds, normal operations, and engine-out procedures.

Even without a fixed minimum number of training hours for the add-on, you still need enough training to meet the applicable standards safely.

Private or Commercial Level?

Think carefully about timing. If you add multi-engine privileges to a private pilot certificate and later earn a commercial certificate in a single-engine airplane, the commercial privilege does not automatically include multi-engine operations.

Career-focused pilots often wait and add multi-engine privileges at the commercial level. That way, the rating supports the kind of flying they plan to do professionally.

The best sequence depends on your goals, budget, and training plan.

If you are building a professional path, map the timing against your commercial pilot timeline before spending money on a checkride that may not match your next certificate goal.

Instrument Considerations

If you already hold an instrument rating, ask your instructor how multi-engine instrument privileges will be handled during training and testing. Multi-engine instrument operations may require demonstrating instrument skills with one engine inoperative.

This is one reason many pilots complete the instrument rating before adding multi-engine privileges. It can make the training path cleaner and reduce duplicate testing later.

What Training Covers

Multi-engine training is heavy on systems and emergencies. Expect to study:

  • Critical engine concepts
  • Vmc and minimum control speed
  • Accelerate-stop and accelerate-go performance
  • Single-engine climb performance
  • Feathering and unfeathering
  • Fuel, electrical, hydraulic, and landing gear systems
  • Normal and short-field takeoffs and landings
  • Engine failure after takeoff
  • Single-engine approaches

The flying can be demanding because the airplane moves faster and there is more to manage. Good checklist discipline becomes even more important.

Endorsements You May Encounter

Many twins also meet the definition of complex aircraft because they have retractable landing gear, flaps, and a controllable-pitch propeller. Some may also require high-performance training depending on engine horsepower.

Do not assume the rating covers every related requirement. Confirm which endorsements you need for the specific aircraft.

Cost Expectations

Multi-engine training is usually expensive because light twins cost more to operate than single-engine trainers. The final cost depends on aircraft rental, instructor rates, ground training, checkride fees, local fuel costs, and how prepared you are.

The best way to control cost is to study before the first lesson. Show up knowing the flows, V-speeds, systems, and emergency profiles. You do not want to learn basic memory items while the Hobbs meter is running in a twin.

The Big Takeaway

A multi-engine rating is a valuable step, but it is not just a paperwork add-on. It teaches a different level of planning and aircraft control. If you train seriously, it can make you a sharper, more disciplined pilot in any airplane.
