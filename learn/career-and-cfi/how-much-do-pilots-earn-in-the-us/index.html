US Pilot Salary Data: How to Read BLS Pay

Learn how to read US pilot salary data, including BLS categories, airline and commercial pilot differences, state variation, and career factors.

Diego Suarez - Updated 2026-04-28 - 4 min read

US pilot salary data is useful, but it needs careful reading. "Pilot" is not one job. A flight instructor, charter pilot, regional airline first officer, cargo captain, corporate pilot, military pilot, and major airline wide-body captain can all have very different pay.

The broad pattern is simple: pilots generally earn more as they gain experience, certificates, flight time, aircraft complexity, and responsibility. But the path is not automatic, and the early years can be financially tight.

For a job-type overview, see how much pilots make. If the question is specifically about whether a private pilot certificate leads to pay, read private pilot salary before assuming a recreational certificate creates a flying job. For exact numbers, check the latest BLS and employer or contract sources directly.

Airline Pilots vs. Commercial Pilots

In everyday conversation, people often use "commercial pilot" to mean any pilot who flies for money. In the FAA certificate sense, a commercial pilot is someone who holds a commercial pilot certificate and may be paid for certain flying operations.

Airline pilots usually need an Airline Transport Pilot certificate or restricted ATP privileges, plus aircraft-specific training and company qualification. Scheduled airline flying tends to pay more than many non-airline commercial pilot jobs, especially at major airlines and later in the career.

Commercial pilot jobs outside the airlines can include charter flying, aerial survey, air tours, pipeline patrol, agricultural operations, banner towing, cargo, firefighting support, and other specialized work. Some of these jobs pay modestly at the entry level. Others can become strong careers with experience.

Why Average Salary Numbers Can Mislead

Average salary figures can be useful, but they hide important details.

First, job titles are often mixed together. A salary website may combine instructors, charter pilots, airline first officers, and captains under one "pilot" category.

Second, seniority matters. A first-year pilot and a senior captain may work for the same airline but live in completely different pay worlds.

Third, location matters. Cost of living, local demand, company base, and type of operation can change the practical value of a salary.

Fourth, benefits matter. Retirement contributions, health insurance, per diem, travel privileges, training pay, bonuses, and schedule quality can make one job more valuable than another even if the base pay looks similar.

Use wage tables, salary websites, and forum posts as starting points, not as financial guarantees. For a real decision, verify employer pay scales, contract language, location, schedule, and benefits.

Entry-Level Pilot Earnings

Many new professional pilots start as flight instructors because instructing is one of the most common ways to build hours. Instructor pay is usually lower than airline pay, and income may depend on student volume, weather, aircraft availability, and whether the instructor is paid hourly or salaried.

Other entry-level jobs may include survey flying, skydiving operations, glider towing, or small cargo work. These jobs can build experience, but students should not assume the first flying job will quickly repay all training costs.

The early-career goal is usually to build safe, legal, high-quality flight time while continuing to learn.

Mid-Career and Airline Earnings

As pilots move into more advanced aircraft and higher-responsibility jobs, pay can rise significantly. Regional airline captains, major airline first officers, corporate jet pilots, and cargo pilots may all see stronger income than the first training jobs.

At the senior end, airline captains flying larger aircraft can earn very high incomes. But that level usually comes after years of training, time building, company seniority, upgrades, recurrent training, medical qualification, and schedule tradeoffs.

State and Employer Differences

Pilot earnings also differ by state and employer. Aviation-heavy states, large airline bases, cargo hubs, military and government operations, and high-demand corporate markets can all influence pay.

Still, do not choose a training path based on one state's average wage table alone. Pilots move, commute, bid bases, change employers, and sometimes trade pay for quality of life.

The Real Career Question

Instead of asking only, "How much do pilots earn?" ask:

  • What certificate and ratings do I need?
  • How much will training cost?
  • How long will it take to build experience?
  • What jobs are realistic at each stage?
  • Where am I willing to live or commute?
  • What schedule can I tolerate?
  • How important are benefits and retirement?

Also ask how long you can support yourself during the first stage. Training debt, moving costs, medical appointments, and checkrides all happen before the larger paychecks. A conservative plan assumes the first flying job is a bridge, not the destination. That mindset helps you stay patient.

Flying can be financially rewarding, but the timeline matters. A strong pilot career is built in stages. Treat salary as one part of the plan, not the whole plan.
