How to Get an Airline Transport Pilot Certificate

Learn the ATP path from private pilot through instrument, commercial, multi-engine, flight time, ATP CTP, testing, and the practical test.

Diego Suarez - Updated 2026-04-27 - 4 min read
On this page Step 1: Earn a Private Pilot Certificate Step 2: Add an Instrument Rating Step 3: Earn a Commercial Pilot Certificate Step 4: Add Multi-Engine Experience

The airline transport pilot certificate, or ATP, is the highest level of FAA pilot certification. It is the certificate associated with airline captain authority when paired with the required aircraft, operation, training, and employer qualifications.

The path is long, but it is not complicated when you break it into stages.

Step 1: Earn a Private Pilot Certificate

The private pilot certificate is the foundation. You learn aircraft control, navigation, weather basics, regulations, emergency procedures, and pilot-in-command decision-making. Pilots usually start with a student pilot certificate before solo.

Private pilot privileges do not allow you to fly for compensation in the normal sense, but they give you the base certificate needed for most advanced ratings and certificates.

Step 2: Add an Instrument Rating

An instrument rating teaches you to fly by reference to instruments and operate under IFR. This is essential for professional flying.

Instrument training builds a more disciplined scan, better weather judgment, stronger ATC communication, and the ability to fly approaches in lower visibility. It also supports later commercial and airline training.

Step 3: Earn a Commercial Pilot Certificate

The commercial certificate raises the precision standard and allows certain paid flying privileges. You will refine maneuvers, planning, aircraft control, and risk management.

For many pilots, commercial training is also where the career path becomes real. You are moving from “I can fly” toward “I can fly to a professional standard.”

Step 4: Add Multi-Engine Experience

Airline aircraft have more than one engine, and multi-engine training is a major step. You learn asymmetric thrust, engine-out procedures, higher workload, and time-sensitive decision-making.

Some pilots complete commercial multi-engine training. Others add multi-engine after the commercial certificate. Either way, multi-engine proficiency matters for airline-track pilots.

Step 5: Build Flight Time

The ATP requires substantial aeronautical experience. The unrestricted ATP path is commonly associated with 1,500 hours total time plus specific categories of cross-country, night, instrument, and pilot-in-command time.

Many pilots build time by instructing. Others use cargo, survey, charter, military, corporate, or other commercial flying jobs. The best time-building path is not just the one that adds hours most quickly. It should also build judgment, professionalism, and varied experience.

Step 6: Understand Restricted ATP Options

Some pilots may qualify for a restricted ATP through military experience or approved aviation degree pathways. The R-ATP can allow eligible pilots to serve as airline second in command before meeting every unrestricted ATP requirement.

R-ATP eligibility is detailed and paperwork-sensitive. If you are choosing a school partly for this reason, verify the FAA authorization and exact requirements before enrolling.

Step 7: Complete ATP CTP

Before taking the ATP knowledge test, applicants generally complete the Airline Transport Pilot Certification Training Program, known as ATP CTP.

ATP CTP includes advanced ground training and simulator training intended to prepare pilots for the airline environment. Topics commonly include high-altitude operations, turbine aircraft, crew resource management, advanced aerodynamics, weather, and air carrier operations.

Step 8: Pass the ATP Knowledge Test

After ATP CTP, you can take the ATP knowledge test. Treat it as more than a written hurdle. The topics support airline-level decision-making and systems awareness.

Keep your documentation organized: ATP CTP completion, knowledge test results, logbook totals, medical qualification, and certificate records.

Step 9: Pass the ATP Practical Test

The ATP practical test evaluates whether you can operate to ATP standards. Depending on the pathway, it may be connected with a type rating or airline training program.

Expect high standards for procedures, crew coordination, aircraft control, systems knowledge, and judgment. ATP-level flying is not about raw stick-and-rudder skill alone. It is about disciplined professional operation.

How Long Does It Take?

Timelines vary widely. Weather, money, training pace, job availability, hiring cycles, and personal circumstances all matter. Some pilots move quickly through full-time training and high-utilization jobs. Others take years while working around life and finances.

Do not measure your value as a pilot by someone else’s timeline. Build skill and experience honestly.

Final Takeaway

The ATP path is a ladder: private, instrument, commercial, multi-engine, flight time, ATP CTP, knowledge test, practical test. If you protect your medical, train consistently, keep clean records, and make conservative decisions, each step supports the next one.
