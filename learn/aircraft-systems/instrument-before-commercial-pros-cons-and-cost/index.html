Instrument Before Commercial Training?

Should you earn your instrument rating before commercial pilot training? Compare the pros, cons, cost factors, and career effects of each path.

Diego Suarez - Updated 2026-04-28 - 4 min read
On this page What the Two Paths Mean Why Instrument First Often Makes Sense Downsides of Instrument First Why Commercial First Can Work

Many pilots reach the same training fork after private pilot: should I earn the instrument rating first, or move toward the commercial certificate and add instrument later?

There is no single right answer. The better choice depends on your budget, schedule, career goal, local school structure, aircraft access, and how consistently you can train.

If you are still mapping the certificate sequence, compare this with the commercial pilot timeline and the step-by-step guide on how to get an instrument rating.

What the Two Paths Mean

An instrument rating teaches you to fly by reference to instruments and operate under IFR when you meet the requirements. It adds major weather and navigation capability, but it also requires regular practice to stay sharp.

A commercial pilot certificate allows you to be paid for certain flying operations, within the privileges and limitations of the certificate and the rules that apply to the operation. Without an instrument rating in the same category and class, commercial airplane privileges can be limited in important ways, including carrying passengers for hire at night or on cross-country flights beyond 50 nautical miles.

That limitation is one reason many career-focused pilots choose instrument first.

Why Instrument First Often Makes Sense

Instrument training can make your commercial path more efficient because the time you spend flying for the rating also builds total time. You are not only adding a qualification; you are becoming a more capable cross-country pilot while moving toward the total time needed for commercial training.

Instrument first can also improve your decision-making. You spend more time studying weather, approaches, alternates, clearances, avionics, and cockpit management. Even when you later fly VFR, those skills make you more disciplined.

For pilots aiming at charter, corporate, airline, or other professional tracks, the instrument rating is usually not optional for long. It becomes part of the expected skill set.

Downsides of Instrument First

The biggest downside is cash flow. Instrument training can be expensive, especially if you train inconsistently, need extra simulator or aircraft time, or have long gaps between lessons.

The second downside is skill fade. If you earn the rating and then spend months building VFR time without practicing IFR procedures, your legal status and practical proficiency can diverge. Instrument skills are perishable.

If you choose instrument first, plan to keep using it. Schedule practice approaches, simulator sessions, or instruction so the rating does not become a logbook item you are nervous to use.

Why Commercial First Can Work

Commercial first can be attractive if your immediate goal is to sharpen aircraft control, build confidence, and move toward work that does not require instrument privileges. Commercial training emphasizes precision, energy management, performance, and professional standards.

It can also spread costs differently. Some pilots prefer to build time and complete commercial maneuvers while saving for instrument training later.

This path can make sense for a pilot with a clear local opportunity that fits the rules, strong VFR weather, and a plan to add instrument soon after.

Downsides of Commercial First

The fine print matters. A commercial certificate without an instrument rating can leave you with meaningful limitations. Some jobs, insurance requirements, aircraft types, and operators may expect or require instrument capability.

You may also end up paying more later if your instrument knowledge and radio procedures have gone stale. Instead of building on fresh training, you may need extra time to rebuild scan, approach briefing, and IFR cockpit flow.

Commercial first is not wrong, but it should be chosen intentionally, not because the instrument rating feels intimidating.

Cost Factors to Compare

Exact prices change by location, aircraft, instructor rate, fuel, simulator availability, checkride scheduling, and how prepared you are. Instead of trusting a single advertised number, compare the cost drivers. For a broader budgeting framework, see how much it costs to become a pilot.

  • Aircraft hourly rate.
  • Instructor hourly rate.
  • Simulator or training device availability.
  • Ground training support.
  • Checkride and knowledge test fees.
  • Training frequency.
  • Weather delays.
  • Examiner availability.
  • How much cross-country time you already have.

Training twice a week with preparation usually costs less than training once every few weeks and relearning each lesson.

Part 61 vs Part 141

Part 61 training is often more flexible. It can work well for pilots with work schedules, local instructors, or pay-as-you-go needs.

Part 141 programs are more structured and may allow reduced minimum flight time in approved courses, but they also require you to follow the approved syllabus. Some academy or university programs combine ratings in a sequence that may effectively decide the order for you.

Ask the school how their syllabus handles instrument and commercial together, how much simulator time is available, how checkride scheduling works, and what students actually spend from start to finish.

A Practical Decision Rule

If your goal is professional flying and you can fund consistent training, instrument first is often the cleaner path. It builds capability, supports later commercial privileges, and keeps you aligned with the skills most employers expect.

If your budget requires a slower path, commercial first can work, but you should understand the limitations and set a clear plan for the instrument rating.

The worst option is drifting. Pick the order that fits your reality, then train consistently. In aviation, momentum saves money, protects proficiency, and keeps the next step from becoming vague.
