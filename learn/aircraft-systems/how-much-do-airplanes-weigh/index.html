How Much Do Airplanes Weigh? MTOW Explained

Learn how much different airplanes weigh, from training aircraft to airliners, and why maximum takeoff weight matters for pilots.

Diego Suarez - Updated 2026-04-28 - 4 min read
On this page What Maximum Takeoff Weight Means Light Training Airplanes Turboprops and Private Jets Airline and Cargo Aircraft

Airplanes can weigh less than a small car or more than a fully loaded building on wings. A light training airplane may have a maximum takeoff weight around a few thousand pounds. A large airliner can be hundreds of thousands of pounds.

For pilots, the useful number is not just "how heavy is the airplane?" The important number is how heavy the airplane is allowed to be for a specific operation.

That is where maximum takeoff weight comes in.

If you are learning this for flight training, connect the big-picture numbers here with the practical math in airplane weight and balance.

What Maximum Takeoff Weight Means

Maximum takeoff weight, or MTOW, is the maximum weight at which an aircraft is approved to begin a takeoff. It includes the empty airplane, fuel, oil, passengers, baggage, cargo, and any other load.

MTOW is not a suggestion. It is a limit tied to aircraft certification, performance, structure, runway needs, climb capability, braking, and controllability.

A pilot may also need to consider landing weight, ramp weight, zero fuel weight, baggage compartment limits, and center of gravity. Weight is not one number. It is a system of limits.

Light Training Airplanes

Small piston airplanes are the easiest place to build intuition. A typical four-seat trainer or personal airplane often has an MTOW in the low thousands of pounds.

Examples in this class include aircraft like the Piper Cherokee, Cessna 172, Cirrus SR22, and Diamond DA42. The exact weight depends on model and configuration, but these airplanes are light compared with turbine aircraft.

Even here, weight matters. Four seats do not always mean four adults, full fuel, and bags. Useful load is the number that tells the truth. A student pilot should learn early that fuel, passengers, and baggage are tradeoffs. The same payload reality affects ownership decisions, which is one reason small aircraft cost is about more than the purchase price.

Turboprops and Private Jets

Turboprops and light jets move into much higher weights. A single-engine turboprop may weigh several times more than a piston trainer. Light and midsize jets can reach into the tens of thousands of pounds.

These aircraft carry more fuel, stronger structures, pressurization systems, heavier engines, advanced avionics, and more passenger comfort equipment. They also operate at higher speeds and altitudes, so performance planning becomes more involved.

At this level, pilots are thinking about runway length, climb gradients, balanced field concepts, obstacle clearance, and fuel planning with a much narrower margin for casual estimating.

Airline and Cargo Aircraft

Commercial airliners are in a different category. Narrow-body jets such as common short- and medium-haul airliners can weigh well over 100,000 pounds at maximum takeoff weight. Large wide-body jets and dedicated cargo aircraft can weigh many hundreds of thousands of pounds.

The largest passenger and cargo aircraft are engineering projects built around enormous lift, fuel, engine thrust, landing gear strength, and runway requirements.

For passengers, that weight is invisible. For pilots and dispatchers, it drives performance planning on every flight.

Why Weight Changes Performance

A heavier airplane needs more runway to accelerate and take off. It climbs more slowly. It may have a higher stall speed. It burns more fuel. It may need different flap settings, speeds, or runway performance calculations.

Weight also affects landing. A heavy aircraft needs more distance to stop and may have higher approach speeds. In some airplanes, landing shortly after takeoff may require fuel burn or special planning if above maximum landing weight.

In a training airplane, the lesson is the same even if the numbers are smaller. Heavy, hot, and high is a serious combination. Add a short runway or obstacles, and the airplane may not perform the way a student expects.

Weight and Balance Go Together

An airplane can be under maximum weight and still unsafe if the center of gravity is outside limits. Weight tells you how much load the airplane carries. Balance tells you where that load sits.

That is why pilots calculate both. Two passengers in the front seats are different from one pilot plus heavy baggage in the rear. Fuel burn can also shift the balance during flight.

Student-Pilot Takeaway

Do not memorize airplane weights as trivia. Learn what the numbers do.

Before each flight, ask:

  • What is the maximum allowed weight?
  • What is the actual takeoff weight?
  • Is the center of gravity inside limits?
  • How does this flight's weight affect runway, climb, and landing performance?
  • Will the airplane remain inside limits after fuel burn?

Airplanes can be impressively light or unbelievably heavy. Either way, the pilot's job is the same: know the limits, do the math, and never ask the airplane to carry more than it safely can.
