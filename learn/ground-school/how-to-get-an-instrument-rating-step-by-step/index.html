How to Get an Instrument Rating Step by Step

Learn how to get an instrument rating, including eligibility, ground training, flight requirements, knowledge test, checkride, and currency.

Diego Suarez - Updated 2026-04-27 - 3 min read
On this page Step 1: Confirm Eligibility Step 2: Choose Part 61 or Part 141 Training Step 3: Start Ground Training Step 4: Build the Required Experience

An instrument rating lets a pilot operate under instrument flight rules and fly by reference to instruments when outside visual references are limited. It is one of the most valuable ratings a private pilot can add.

Even if you mostly fly in good weather, instrument training improves your scan, communication, weather planning, navigation, and discipline.

Step 1: Confirm Eligibility

For an airplane instrument rating, you generally need to hold at least a private pilot certificate or be applying for one at the same time. You must meet age, English, training, endorsement, and medical eligibility requirements.

Some pilots may be able to operate under BasicMed within its limits, but medical eligibility can be nuanced. Verify your situation before planning training or a checkride.

Step 2: Choose Part 61 or Part 141 Training

Instrument training can be completed under Part 61 or Part 141. Part 61 is often more flexible. Part 141 uses an approved syllabus and may have different minimums.

The best choice depends on your schedule, budget, location, and learning style. A structured program can help some students move efficiently. A flexible instructor can work better for pilots balancing work and family.

Step 3: Start Ground Training

Instrument ground training covers IFR regulations, weather, charts, procedures, aircraft systems, navigation, approach briefings, holding, lost communications, alternate planning, and decision-making.

Do not treat ground school as written-test trivia. IFR flying is procedure-heavy. The concepts you study on the ground become the instructions you must execute in the airplane.

Step 4: Build the Required Experience

Under the standard Part 61 airplane path, instrument rating applicants plan around instrument time, cross-country experience, instructor training, and specific IFR cross-country work. Some simulator or training device time may count if it meets FAA requirements.

The exact hour rules depend on the training path and equipment used, so track time carefully. Make sure your logbook entries clearly identify actual or simulated instrument time, approaches, holds, cross-country time, and instructor training.

Messy records create stress when checkride time arrives.

Step 5: Use Simulation Wisely

Approved aviation training devices can be useful and cost-effective for instrument training. They help with procedures, scan, approaches, holds, and avionics flow.

But simulation should support airplane proficiency, not replace it mentally. The real airplane adds weather, ATC pace, turbulence, workload, and consequences. Use both environments intentionally.

Step 6: Pass the Knowledge Test

The instrument rating knowledge test covers IFR procedures, weather, navigation, regulations, charts, and systems. You need the proper authorization and an FAA Tracking Number before taking it.

Study to understand, not just pass. If you memorize questions without understanding approach minimums, alternate rules, or weather products, the checkride and real IFR flying will expose the gap.

Step 7: Prepare for the Checkride

The practical test includes an oral exam and flight portion. The examiner will evaluate your ability to plan, brief, fly, and manage IFR procedures safely.

Expect to discuss weather, alternates, lost communications, aircraft equipment, navigation systems, approach plates, departure procedures, enroute charts, and decision-making.

In flight, you may perform approaches, holds, unusual attitude recovery, partial-panel work, navigation, and missed approaches. The exact tasks follow the applicable standards.

Step 8: Stay Current After You Pass

An instrument rating is only useful if you maintain both legal currency and practical proficiency. Instrument currency commonly involves recent approaches, holding, and intercepting and tracking courses. If you fall outside the allowed window, you may need an instrument proficiency check.

Legal currency is not always enough. If you have not flown actual IFR in months, train with an instructor before launching into low weather.

Final Takeaway

The instrument rating is a major skill upgrade. It gives you more flexibility, but it also demands precision and humility. Train steadily, keep clean records, brief every procedure, and remember that good IFR pilots are conservative weather decision-makers. It is also a key step for pilots who later pursue an airline transport pilot certificate.
