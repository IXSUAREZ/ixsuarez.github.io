Simply Endorsed Private Pilot Checkride Endorsements

The private pilot checkride path usually combines more than one endorsement step. In the common private ASEL workflow, instructors think in terms of the applicant's knowledge-test preparation, review of written-test deficiencies, recent practical-test preparation, and the final private pilot practical-test recommendation.

Simply Endorsed reflects that reality by opening the private airplane initial checkride bundle with A.37 as the primary endorsement and A.36, A.2, and A.1 as the commonly included companions. That keeps the written-prep history, the Airman Knowledge Test Report deficiency review, and the 2-calendar-month practical-test recency endorsement in one place so instructors can confirm the full recommendation path before the applicant meets the examiner.

How the private checkride bundle fits together

The private pilot recommendation path is a good example of why a searchable bundle is more useful than a bare list of endorsements. A.36 is the knowledge-test endorsement that supports written-test readiness. A.2 is the deficiency-review signoff used after the knowledge test when the instructor reviews the missed subject areas shown on the Airman Knowledge Test Report. A.1 covers recent practical-test preparation under 14 CFR 61.39, and A.37 is the private pilot flight proficiency or practical-test endorsement tied to the private certificate standards themselves.

Instructors do not always touch those entries on the same day, which is exactly why they are easy to lose track of in a busy training schedule. Simply Endorsed groups them into the practical workflow an instructor actually wants to audit before recommending a student for the private pilot checkride.

Common private pilot checkride endorsements

A.36

Aeronautical knowledge test

Shows the applicant completed the required ground training or review and is ready for the private pilot knowledge test.

14 CFR §§ 61.35(a)(1), 61.103(d), and 61.105

A.2

Review of deficiencies identified on airman knowledge test

Shows the instructor reviewed the missed knowledge-test areas listed on the Airman Knowledge Test Report.

14 CFR § 61.39(a)(6)(iii), as required

A.1

Prerequisites for practical test

Confirms the applicant got checkride prep within the last 2 calendar months and is ready for the practical test.

14 CFR § 61.39(a)(6)(i) and (ii)

A.37

Flight proficiency/practical test

Shows the applicant completed the required training and is proficient for the private pilot flight proficiency or practical test.

14 CFR §§ 61.103(f), 61.107(b), and 61.109

Why instructors use the bundle view

The practical benefit is speed and consistency. Before a private pilot checkride, an instructor can open the bundle, review which endorsements are usually in play, and check whether the applicant still has a current A.1 window, a completed deficiency review, and the correct private pilot practical-test recommendation. That is faster than trying to remember the endorsement sequence from memory or hunting through AC 61-65 paragraph by paragraph during the last stage of training.

FAQ: private pilot checkride endorsements

Does every private pilot checkride path include A.2?
A.2 is used when the instructor reviews the missed subject areas shown on the Airman Knowledge Test Report. If there were written-test deficiencies to review, it matters in the practical-test recommendation path.
How long is A.1 valid?
A.1 covers recent practical-test preparation and is tied to a 2-calendar-month window. That is one reason instructors like to check the bundle again close to the checkride date.
Is A.36 still part of the checkride story after the written is complete?
Yes. A.36 is the knowledge-test endorsement that supports written-test readiness, while the rest of the bundle helps the instructor confirm the applicant also completed the later deficiency-review and practical-test recommendation steps.
Does this guide replace IACRA or examiner preferences?
No. This guide is a reference view of the common AC 61-65K endorsement flow. Instructors still need to verify the applicant's actual IACRA record, practical-test eligibility, and any current examiner or school workflow requirements.
