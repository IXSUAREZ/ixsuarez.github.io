Do Airline Pilots Need a College Degree?

Learn how airline pilot college degree preferences differ from FAA certificate rules, and how to plan education around hiring risk.

Diego Suarez - Updated 2026-04-27 - 4 min read
On this page Airline Pilot College Degree vs FAA Requirements Why Airlines May Value a Degree Where the Degree Question Matters Most Aviation Degree vs Traditional Flight School for Airline Goals

You do not need a college degree to earn an FAA pilot certificate, but an airline pilot college degree can still matter as an employer preference. That distinction is important. The FAA controls certification; airlines control hiring standards.

This article focuses on the career and employer side of the question. If your goal is the broader "Can I become a pilot without college?" path, start with the companion guide on becoming a pilot without a degree.

Airline hiring expectations vary with company policy, applicant supply, training background, military experience, and the hiring market. That is why the better question is not just "Do pilots need college?" It is "What kind of airline or professional flying job am I trying to reach, and what will that employer value when I apply?" The answer can vary between instruction, charter, corporate, cargo, and airline operations, so it helps to understand the difference between Part 91, 121, and 135.

Airline Pilot College Degree vs FAA Requirements

The FAA sets certificate requirements. Employers set hiring preferences.

To become a pilot, you must meet the FAA requirements for the certificate or rating you are pursuing. Those requirements can include age, English language ability, training, flight time, endorsements, knowledge tests, practical tests, and medical certification.

A college degree is not the basic FAA requirement for earning pilot certificates or ratings.

Airlines and other employers may have different preferences. Some may prefer or historically have preferred a degree, especially for competitive positions. Those preferences can change with hiring cycles, labor markets, and company policy.

Why Airlines May Value a Degree

A degree can signal that an applicant completed a long program, managed deadlines, wrote clearly, and handled a structured workload. It can also help a pilot build a backup career if medical certification, hiring cycles, or family needs interrupt flying.

That does not mean a degree makes someone a safer pilot by itself. Flight time, training quality, checkride performance, professionalism, decision-making, and interview preparation still matter. A weak training record is not fixed by a diploma.

Where the Degree Question Matters Most

The airline pilot college degree question matters most when you are comparing long-term career paths, not when you are deciding whether you can start flight training.

Some pilots build strong careers without aviation degrees. Some earn non-aviation degrees as a backup. Some choose university aviation programs that combine academics and flight training.

There is no single perfect path. Cost, timeline, career goals, financial aid, location, training quality, and backup plans all matter.

Aviation Degree vs Traditional Flight School for Airline Goals

A university aviation program may combine flight training with academic coursework in meteorology, safety, aircraft systems, human factors, and aviation operations. It can provide structure and a broader college experience.

Traditional flight schools usually focus more directly on certificates and ratings. They may be faster and less expensive, depending on the school and the student.

Neither path is automatically better. The right choice depends on your budget, learning style, career goal, and need for a degree.

Does the Major Matter for an Airline Pilot College Degree?

If you decide to earn a degree, the major may not matter as much as students assume. Aviation degrees can be useful, but a non-aviation degree can provide a backup career path if medical, financial, or hiring conditions change.

Business, engineering, communications, computer science, education, or another field can all show discipline and provide options outside the cockpit.

Choose a degree you can finish and use.

Cost Matters

Flight training is expensive. College is expensive. Combining them can be very expensive.

Before choosing a program, compare total cost, aircraft rental rates, instructor availability, completion timelines, financing, refund policies, and what happens if you lose medical eligibility or need to pause training. For the flight-training side of that decision, compare practical ways to pay for flight school.

Do not assume the most expensive route is the most professional route.

Medical and Backup Planning

Pilot careers can be affected by medical certification, hiring cycles, family needs, and finances. A degree can be part of a backup plan, but it is not the only backup plan.

Some pilots choose a non-aviation degree so they have a second professional path. Others choose aviation because they want a structured college environment and access to university flight training. Both choices can be reasonable if the student understands the tradeoffs.

The risky choice is borrowing heavily without understanding the total cost, career timeline, and what happens if training takes longer than expected.

A Practical Decision Process

Ask yourself:

  • Do I want to fly for fun or as a career?
  • Do I need a degree for the employers I am targeting?
  • Can I afford college and flight training together?
  • Would a non-aviation degree give me a better backup plan?
  • Which option lets me train consistently and safely?

Then talk to working pilots, instructors, and schools before committing.

Bottom Line

You do not need a college degree to become a pilot under FAA certificate rules. For airline and other competitive career paths, a degree may still help depending on employer preferences, your background, and the hiring market.

Build the path around current requirements, realistic costs, and a backup plan. The goal is not just to start training. The goal is to finish and be employable for the kind of flying you want to do. Do not treat any employer preference as permanent; hiring standards change with the market.
