The Airspeed Indicator: How It Works and What It Does

Learn how the airspeed indicator works, how pitot-static pressure creates an airspeed reading, and why airspeed matters in every phase of flight.

Diego Suarez - Updated 2026-04-28 - 4 min read
On this page What the Airspeed Indicator Measures The Pitot-Static System Reading the Colored Arcs Why Airspeed Matters

The airspeed indicator is one of the first instruments a student pilot learns to trust. It tells you how fast the airplane is moving through the air, and that information affects takeoff, climb, cruise, maneuvering, approach, and landing.

An airplane does not care how fast it is moving over the ground when it comes to lift. It cares about airflow over the wing. That is why indicated airspeed is so important in the cockpit.

What the Airspeed Indicator Measures

The airspeed indicator does not directly measure speed like a car speedometer. It measures pressure difference.

The instrument compares two pressures:

  • Pitot pressure, which comes from air being rammed into the pitot tube as the airplane moves forward.
  • Static pressure, which comes from relatively undisturbed outside air.

The faster the airplane moves through the air, the greater the difference between those two pressures. The airspeed indicator converts that pressure difference into a speed reading.

The Pitot-Static System

The airspeed indicator is part of the pitot-static system. The pitot tube usually faces forward into the airflow. The static port is usually located on the fuselage or another area where it can sense outside air pressure with minimal disturbance.

If either side of the system is blocked, the airspeed reading can become unreliable. That is why pitot heat, static port checks, and preflight inspection matter.

A blocked pitot tube or static port is not just an instrument problem. It can become an aircraft-control problem if the pilot believes a false airspeed indication.

Reading the Colored Arcs

Most analog airspeed indicators have colored arcs and markings. Glass panels show the same information in a different format.

The white arc is the normal flap operating range. The lower end is the stall speed in landing configuration. The upper end is the maximum speed for full flap operation.

The green arc is the normal operating range. The lower end is the clean stall speed. The upper end is often the maximum structural cruising speed.

The yellow arc is the caution range. You should only operate there in smooth air and with care.

The red line is never-exceed speed. Do not exceed it.

These speeds are aircraft-specific. The pilot's operating handbook or airplane flight manual is the authority for your airplane.

Why Airspeed Matters

Airspeed affects stall margin. If the airplane slows too much at a high enough angle of attack, the wing can stall. The exact stall speed depends on configuration, bank angle, weight, load factor, and other conditions.

Airspeed also affects flap and gear operation. Extending flaps or landing gear above their limits can damage components or overstress the airplane.

Navigation depends on airspeed too. Planning time, fuel, and groundspeed requires understanding the difference between indicated airspeed, true airspeed, and groundspeed.

Maneuvering is another factor. Higher airspeed makes control inputs more effective and can increase structural loads. That is why turbulence penetration speeds and maneuvering speeds matter.

Indicated, True, and Groundspeed

Indicated airspeed is what you see on the instrument. It is most useful for aircraft control and limitations.

True airspeed is your speed through the air mass after correcting for altitude and temperature effects. It becomes more important in cruise planning.

Groundspeed is your speed over the ground. Wind affects it. You can have a high indicated airspeed and a low groundspeed in a strong headwind, or the opposite with a tailwind.

For training, keep the roles clear: indicated airspeed for flying the airplane, groundspeed for travel progress, and true airspeed for performance planning.

Common Student Mistakes

One common mistake is chasing airspeed with abrupt pitch changes. The better habit is to set the right attitude, add the right power, trim, and then let the airplane stabilize. If you constantly jab at the controls because the needle moved five knots, the airplane usually gets less stable.

Another mistake is looking at airspeed without context. A slow airspeed on final is important, but so are pitch attitude, power setting, wind, configuration, and sink rate. A fast airspeed in turbulence also matters because structural loads can build quickly.

During training, say the important numbers out loud until they become familiar. Rotate speed, best-rate climb speed, flap speeds, approach speed, maneuvering speed, and never-exceed speed should not feel like trivia. They are working numbers for the airplane you are flying.

Student-Pilot Takeaway

The airspeed indicator is not just a number to glance at on final. It is a pressure instrument that supports safe aircraft control.

Learn the colored arcs, know your airplane's important V-speeds, and understand what a pitot-static failure can do. Airspeed discipline is one of the cleanest signs of a pilot who is ahead of the airplane.
