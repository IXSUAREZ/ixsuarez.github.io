Deadstick Landings: How Pilots Handle Engine-Out Emergencies

A practical guide to deadstick landings, engine-out priorities, best glide, landing-site selection, and emergency training for pilots.

Diego Suarez - Updated 2026-04-27 - 4 min read
On this page First Priority: Airspeed Pick a Landing Area Troubleshoot Without Stopping the Glide Communicate and Secure

A deadstick landing is a landing made without engine power. In a training airplane, that usually means the pilot must glide to a suitable landing area and manage energy carefully all the way to touchdown.

The topic sounds dramatic, but the training lesson is calm and practical: fly the airplane, choose a landing site, run the checklist if time allows, and commit to a controlled landing. That same "fly first" priority shows up in everyday airspeed and altitude control.

First Priority: Airspeed

When the engine fails, the first danger is not silence. It is losing airspeed.

Lower the nose as needed and establish the aircraft's recommended best glide speed. That speed gives you the best glide performance for the aircraft configuration and weight assumptions in the POH/AFM.

If you delay and let the airplane slow toward a stall, you may lose the one thing you still have: control.

Pick a Landing Area

After establishing glide, choose a landing area. A runway is ideal, but a field, road, shoreline, or other open area may be the realistic option.

Do not spend too long searching for perfect terrain while flying away from good-enough terrain. Once you choose a site, keep checking whether you can actually make it.

If a clearly better option appears early, you may change plans. But repeated indecision can burn altitude fast.

Troubleshoot Without Stopping the Glide

If altitude and time allow, run the engine failure checklist. That may include fuel selector, mixture, carb heat if applicable, ignition, primer, and other aircraft-specific items.

Do not let troubleshooting distract you from flying the airplane. The checklist matters, but the airplane still needs airspeed, bank control, and a landing plan.

Communicate and Secure

If time allows, declare an emergency, set the transponder as appropriate, and communicate position and intentions. Then prepare the cabin.

Seat belts tight. Loose items secured. Fuel and electrical items configured according to the checklist. Doors may be unlatched before touchdown if the checklist calls for it.

Use the aircraft's actual emergency procedure, not a generic memory of an article.

The Impossible Turn

An engine failure shortly after takeoff is especially dangerous because altitude is low. Turning back to the runway may look tempting, but it often requires more altitude than pilots expect.

Many instructors teach students to land generally ahead or within a limited arc unless they have enough altitude, training, and a prebriefed plan. The exact decision depends on aircraft, altitude, wind, runway, terrain, and pilot proficiency.

Brief the plan before takeoff so you are not inventing it during the emergency.

Energy Management

Without power, altitude is your stored energy. You can spend it, but you cannot get it back.

Use turns, slips if appropriate, flaps when assured, and configuration changes carefully. Extending flaps too early can make you come up short. Waiting too long can leave you high and fast. Descent planning practice helps here because it teaches you to see whether the airplane is actually making the intended descent profile.

The goal is a controlled touchdown at the lowest safe speed, with the cabin protected as much as possible.

Large Aircraft Can Glide Too

Jets and large aircraft do not fall straight down when engines fail. They glide, though the speeds, systems, and procedures are different.

Famous engine-out events, such as the Air Canada "Gimli Glider," show that deadstick landings can be survivable with skill, crew coordination, and judgment. They also show why fuel management and procedural discipline matter.

Training for Engine Failure

Practice engine-out procedures with a qualified instructor. Simulated failures build habits: pitch for glide, choose a field, plan the pattern, use the checklist, communicate, and manage configuration.

Home simulators can help rehearse flows, but they cannot replace aircraft-specific instruction. The sight picture, stress, drag, wind, and field selection are real-world skills.

Before-Takeoff Brief

A useful emergency response starts before the throttle goes forward. Brief what you will do if the engine fails on the runway, shortly after liftoff, and higher in the climb.

This does not need to be long. Say the minimum runway remaining, the airspeed priority, the landing area ahead, and the altitude where additional options may become available. The goal is to remove hesitation.

During cruise, keep asking where you would land if the engine quit now. That habit keeps your brain ahead of the airplane.

Student Pilot Takeaway

A deadstick landing is not about heroics. It is about priorities.

Fly the airplane. Pick a place. Follow the checklist as time allows. Communicate. Secure. Land under control.

Those habits are built before the engine ever gets quiet. Review the emergency procedures for the airplane you fly, brief your options before takeoff, and practice realistic engine-out scenarios with a qualified instructor.
